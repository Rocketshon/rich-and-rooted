Most applicants walk into the mortgage process thinking the lender is going to get to know them. They won't. What the lender is actually going to do — what an underwriter like I used to be is going to do — is run your file through a short list of ratios, and then spend the rest of the review deciding whether the paperwork supports those numbers. That's it. That's the job.

Which is good news, if you know the ratios before you apply.

## 1. Credit score — the one you already know about

Credit is the ratio everyone's heard of, which is why it tends to get the most attention and the most anxiety. It's also the one most often misunderstood. Two things to know:

- **It is a threshold, not a dial.** Lenders price loans in score bands — 620, 660, 700, 740, 760. Jumping from a 698 to a 702 is worth far more than jumping from a 742 to a 758.
- **Utilization moves fastest.** If you need to raise a score in 60 days, the only reliable lever is credit card balances. Pay them down before the statement date, not the due date.

## 2. Debt-to-income — the ratio nobody teaches you

DTI is a simple fraction: monthly debt obligations ÷ gross monthly income. The mortgage you're applying for is counted *in the numerator*, which is why "I can afford the payment" is not the same as "I qualify."

Most conventional programs cap DTI around 43–45%. FHA tolerates higher with compensating factors. What no one tells first-time buyers: a car loan with 8 months left is treated the same as one with 80 months left. If you can pay off the short ones before applying, you can meaningfully change the math.

> The underwriter doesn't want to know what you *can* afford. They want to know what the file says you've already shown you can afford — repeatedly, on paper, over two years.

## 3. Loan-to-value — the one you can shape

Loan-to-value is the mortgage amount divided by the appraised value of the property. It is the ratio most directly affected by the two things people worry least about: *down payment* and *appraisal*.

A 10% down payment gets you a 90 LTV. A 20% down payment gets you an 80 LTV, and that second figure unlocks better pricing and eliminates private mortgage insurance. The jump between 85 and 80 is often worth a full percentage point on the rate over the life of the loan.

### What actually happens at the desk

When a file hits underwriting, the first thing I did — the first thing any competent underwriter does — was pull all three ratios onto a single sheet and stare at them together. If two of the three were clean, the third could be defended. If two of the three were tight, the file was going to need a story, and the story had to live in the paperwork.

This is the insight that's worth the whole article: **you are not applying for a loan, you are submitting an argument**. The ratios are the argument's structure. The documents are the evidence. If the evidence doesn't point where the ratios need it to, the file gets denied — not because the underwriter is mean, but because the paperwork quietly refused to cooperate.

## A short preparation list

1. Pull your own credit report at least 90 days before applying. Dispute anything inaccurate; pay down revolving utilization.
2. Calculate your DTI honestly with the prospective mortgage payment included. If you're over 40%, your preparation window needs to focus there first.
3. Decide your down payment *before* you fall in love with a specific property. Loan-to-value anxiety is the leading cause of last-minute panic.
4. Gather two years of documentation — W-2s, 1099s, returns, bank statements — in one folder. Label everything. Future you will thank present you.

None of this is magic. It's just the part of the process that happens before you ever sit down with a lender — and the part almost nobody prepares for, because almost nobody tells them it exists.
